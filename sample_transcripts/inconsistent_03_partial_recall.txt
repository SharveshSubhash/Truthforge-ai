DISTRICT COURT OF SINGAPORE
CRIMINAL CASE NO. DC/CC 77/2025

PUBLIC PROSECUTOR v LIM SWEE HUAT

Date of Hearing: 17 February 2025
Coram: District Judge Kenneth Yap Shi Jian
Counsel for Prosecution: Deputy Public Prosecutor Mr. Samuel Cheah Wei Bin
Counsel for Defence: Mr. Fadzil bin Rahmat

CASE SUMMARY:
The accused, a former accounts manager, is charged under s.408 of the Penal
Code for criminal breach of trust as a servant. It is alleged that over a
period from January to August 2024, he misappropriated approximately $47,800
from his employer, Horizon Logistics Pte Ltd, through a series of falsified
petty cash claims. The hearing focuses on several specific withdrawal transactions.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF PW1 (First Prosecution Witness — Ms. Grace Woo Pei
Shan, Finance Director, Horizon Logistics Pte Ltd)

DPP CHEAH: Ms. Grace, can you explain the petty cash system at Horizon Logistics?
PW1: The accounts manager — in this case Mr. Lim — was responsible for
managing the petty cash float and approving reimbursements up to $500.
Anything above $500 required a second approver. He had sole access to
the petty cash box and was the only signatory for amounts under $500.

DPP CHEAH: When did you discover the alleged irregularities?
PW1: In September 2024, during an internal audit triggered by a routine
check. The auditor found 23 petty cash vouchers totalling $47,800 that
were either unsubstantiated or had forged supporting documents — mostly
taxi receipts and hardware store invoices.

DPP CHEAH: Was Mr. Lim the one who approved these vouchers?
PW1: Yes. His signature and staff number appear on all 23 vouchers.

DPP CHEAH: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF PW1

MR. FADZIL: Ms. Grace, were the petty cash vouchers kept in a secure
location accessible to anyone other than Mr. Lim?
PW1: The petty cash box was in a cabinet in the finance room. Mr. Lim
had the key. But the room itself was shared by three finance staff.

MR. FADZIL: So other staff members had access to the finance room but
not the petty cash box?
PW1: That's correct.

MR. FADZIL: Could any of those staff members have had a duplicate key
to the petty cash box without Mr. Lim's knowledge?
PW1: It is possible in theory, but we have no evidence of that.

MR. FADZIL: No further questions.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF PW2 (Second Prosecution Witness — Mr. Ernest Khoo,
External Auditor, BDO Singapore)

DPP CHEAH: Mr. Ernest, what did you find during the audit?
PW2: I was engaged to conduct a forensic audit in September 2024. I
examined 23 petty cash vouchers flagged by the internal audit. Of these,
11 had supporting receipts that appeared to be photocopies of genuine
receipts with amounts altered. Eight had receipts from businesses that
I verified do not exist or have no record of such transactions. Four
had no supporting documentation at all. The total claimed across
all 23 was $47,823.50.

DPP CHEAH: Could you determine when these transactions occurred?
PW2: The voucher dates ranged from 15 January 2024 to 29 August 2024.
The pattern suggests small claims — mostly between $800 and $2,500 —
spread over eight months. This is consistent with a deliberate strategy
to keep individual amounts low.

DPP CHEAH: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF PW2

MR. FADZIL: Mr. Ernest, the vouchers bear Mr. Lim's signature. Is it
possible that someone forged his signature?
PW2: I am not a handwriting expert. I can only say the signatures appear
consistent with other samples of Mr. Lim's signature in employment
records. But I cannot exclude the possibility of forgery.

MR. FADZIL: Did you check whether the forged receipts could have been
created by someone other than Mr. Lim?
PW2: I did not have the tools or mandate to determine who specifically
created the documents. My findings identify the anomalies; the
question of authorship is a separate forensic matter.

MR. FADZIL: No further questions.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF DW1 (The Accused — Mr. Lim Swee Huat)

MR. FADZIL: Mr. Lim, did you falsify these petty cash vouchers?
DW1: No. I approved legitimate petty cash claims. Some of them — I will
admit — I may not have been as rigorous in checking the supporting
documents as I should have been. But I did not fabricate anything.

MR. FADZIL: Do you have any explanation for why 23 vouchers bear your
signature and appear falsified?
DW1: I believe it was another staff member. A colleague named Brandon —
Brandon Seet — he had approached me before about personal financial
difficulties. I recall now that on a few occasions, maybe three or
four times, he asked me to approve claims urgently and I signed
without checking closely because I trusted him.

MR. FADZIL: When did you recall this?
DW1: It came back to me when I reviewed the vouchers in preparation
for this case.

MR. FADZIL: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF DW1

DPP CHEAH: Mr. Lim, your defence is that a colleague named Brandon Seet
deceived you. When was the first time you mentioned Brandon Seet's name
in connection with this matter?
DW1: In my second statement to the police.

DPP CHEAH: Your first statement to the police, given on 4 September 2024,
makes no mention of Brandon Seet at all. You only introduced this name
in a statement given six weeks later on 16 October 2024. Why?
DW1: I needed time to think and piece things together.

DPP CHEAH: Brandon Seet left Horizon Logistics in June 2024 — two months
before the last falsified voucher was dated 29 August 2024. If he was
responsible, how did he create a voucher two months after leaving?
DW1: Perhaps he had prepared them in advance before leaving.

DPP CHEAH: Did you attend any hardware store or take any taxi journeys
that could account for any of the 23 claims?
DW1: Some of them may have been legitimate expenses. I can't remember all
of them now — it was months ago.

DPP CHEAH: You approved 23 claims totalling $47,823. You cannot recall
a single specific occasion or purpose?
DW1: I approve many claims in a month. I don't remember the details of
every one.

DPP CHEAH: No further questions.

COURT: The court notes the following inconsistency in the accused's
evidence: (1) Brandon Seet's name does not appear in the first
police statement and was introduced only six weeks later;
(2) The accused's explanation does not account for vouchers dated
after Seet's departure. The accused further states he cannot recall
any specific transaction from 23 claims totalling nearly $48,000.
The court will take this into account in assessing credibility.

[HEARING ADJOURNED FOR FURTHER EVIDENCE ON 20 FEBRUARY 2025]
