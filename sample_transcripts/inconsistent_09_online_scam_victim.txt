DISTRICT COURT OF SINGAPORE
CRIMINAL CASE NO. DC/CC 218/2025

PUBLIC PROSECUTOR v TAN ENG HWEE

Date of Hearing: 5 May 2025
Coram: District Judge Lee Boon Seng
Counsel for Prosecution: Deputy Public Prosecutor Mr. Alvin Wee Joo Seng
Counsel for Defence: Ms. Farida binte Wahab

CASE SUMMARY:
The accused is charged under s.420 of the Penal Code for cheating. It is
alleged that in August and September 2024, the accused operated an online
scam posing as a seller of concert tickets, defrauding six victims of
amounts between $200 and $1,600. The total alleged fraud is $4,800. The
victims' accounts contain inconsistencies regarding the amounts, dates,
and platform of contact.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF PW1 (First Prosecution Witness — Ms. Nur Aishah
binte Ramlan, victim, 22 years old, student)

DPP WEE: Ms. Aishah, what happened?
PW1: In August 2024, I saw a Carousell listing for two Taylor Swift concert
tickets. I paid $800 by PayLah to the seller. The seller then said there
was an admin fee and asked for another $150. I paid. He then went silent.
I contacted Carousell and they confirmed the account was fake.

DPP WEE: Total amount lost?
PW1: $950.

DPP WEE: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF PW1

MS. FARIDA: Ms. Aishah, your police report, Exhibit D2, states you paid
a total of $800. Today you say $800 plus $150, totalling $950. Which is
correct?
PW1: The report was filed quickly. I forgot to include the admin fee.
$950 is correct.

MS. FARIDA: The report also says you paid via "bank transfer." Today
you say "PayLah." Are these different transactions or the same?
PW1: PayLah is connected to my bank. I transferred via the PayLah app.
I may have said "bank transfer" to the officer — it's effectively the same.

MS. FARIDA: Do you know for certain the seller was this accused?
PW1: The police told me the PayLah account received money from multiple
victims. I identified the account number as the one I sent money to.

MS. FARIDA: But you never met the accused in person?
PW1: No.

MS. FARIDA: No further questions.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF PW2 (Second Prosecution Witness — Mr. Ethan Chan
Yong Ming, victim, 24 years old, accounts executive)

DPP WEE: Mr. Ethan, what happened to you?
PW2: I saw a Telegram message in a group selling two Taylor Swift tickets
for $1,200. I contacted the seller. He said tickets were going fast and
to PayNow immediately. I transferred $1,200. He then said he needed
$400 more for insurance to release the tickets. I refused and demanded
a refund. He disappeared.

DPP WEE: Total amount lost?
PW2: $1,200.

DPP WEE: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF PW2

MS. FARIDA: Mr. Ethan, PW1 says she found the seller on Carousell. You
say you found the seller on Telegram. Are you sure these were the same
seller?
PW2: The police connected the PayNow account to the same person. But I
cannot personally confirm it was the same listing.

MS. FARIDA: The amount PW1 paid — $950 — and your amount — $1,200 —
and the other four victims allegedly paid different amounts. If this was
a fixed ticket price, why are all the amounts different?
PW2: The seller may have quoted different prices to different buyers.
Concert scalpers do this.

MS. FARIDA: No further questions.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF DW1 (The Accused — Mr. Tan Eng Hwee)

MS. FARIDA: Mr. Tan, did you sell fake concert tickets?
DW1: No. I sold my PayNow account and SIM card to a stranger I met at
a coffee shop in July 2024. I needed money. He paid me $300. I gave
him my account credentials and the SIM card. Whatever happened after
that was not my doing.

MS. FARIDA: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF DW1

DPP WEE: Mr. Tan, you sold your bank account and SIM card to a stranger
for $300. Do you know this is an offence under the Payment Services Act?
DW1: I did not know at the time.

DPP WEE: Can you describe this stranger?
DW1: Chinese male, maybe 40s, slightly heavy build. I met him once.
I don't know his name.

DPP WEE: You have no name, no phone number, no description of this buyer?
DW1: No. It was a cash deal.

DPP WEE: The transaction records from your OCBC account, Exhibit P8,
show that between the scam period and 15 October 2024, $2,800 was
transferred from the fraudulent receipts to a second OCBC account also
registered in your name. Can a stranger who bought your SIM and account
also access a second account that you never gave them?
DW1: I... that second account, I also gave to him. I had two accounts.

DPP WEE: This is the first mention of a second account. Your police
statement and Case for the Defence make no reference to a second account.
DW1: I forgot about it.

DPP WEE: No further questions.

COURT: The court notes the following:
(1) Inconsistencies among victims: different platforms (Carousell vs Telegram),
different amounts, different payment methods — though the prosecution
ties them to a common account.
(2) The accused's defence — account sale to a stranger — is undermined
by the second account transfer, which emerged only in cross-examination.
(3) PW1's amount ($800 vs $950) and payment method (bank transfer vs PayLah)
were inconsistent between her police report and court testimony.
