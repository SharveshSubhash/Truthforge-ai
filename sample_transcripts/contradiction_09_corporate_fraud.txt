HIGH COURT OF SINGAPORE
CRIMINAL CASE NO. HC/CC 12/2025

PUBLIC PROSECUTOR v WINSTON LEE BENG HUAT

Date of Hearing: 10 March 2025
Coram: Justice Audrey Lim
Counsel for Prosecution: Senior Deputy Public Prosecutor Mr. Heng Jian Sheng
Counsel for Defence: Mr. David Nair SC and Mr. Kenneth Chew

CASE SUMMARY:
The accused, a former director of Pinnacle Marine Services Pte Ltd, faces
three charges under s.409 of the Penal Code for criminal breach of trust as
an agent, involving misappropriation of S$1.87 million from company accounts
between March 2022 and August 2024. The accused claims the transfers were
authorised for business purposes and denies misappropriation.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF PW1 (First Prosecution Witness — Mr. Albert Wee Chee
Hock, Chief Financial Officer, Pinnacle Marine Services Pte Ltd)

SDPP HENG: Mr. Albert, when did you first discover the alleged irregularities?
PW1: In September 2024, during the preparation of our FY2024 annual
audit. Our external auditors flagged three bank transfers totalling
S$1.87 million to an entity called "Meridian Venture Capital Pte Ltd."
I had no knowledge of any approved investment in Meridian. The transfers
had been authorised using Winston's digital token.

SDPP HENG: Was there any board resolution approving these transfers?
PW1: No. Any investment above S$500,000 requires full board approval
under our articles of association. No board meeting was called for
this purpose. I checked the board minutes thoroughly.

SDPP HENG: Had Pinnacle Marine received any benefit from Meridian
Venture Capital?
PW1: None. When I searched ACRA records, Meridian Venture Capital Pte Ltd
was incorporated on 4 March 2022 — one week before the first transfer
to it. The sole director and shareholder is a Mr. Philip Tan Ah Kow.

SDPP HENG: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF PW1

MR. NAIR SC: Mr. Albert, Pinnacle Marine's articles of association
require board approval for investments above S$500,000. But the three
transactions were each below S$500,000, were they not? Exhibit D8
shows transfers of S$490,000, S$498,500, and S$881,500.
PW1: Two of the three are below S$500,000 on their face. The third
is S$881,500.

MR. NAIR SC: Could the two smaller transactions have been within Mr. Lee's
executive authority as managing director?
PW1: Our internal policy is that the MD may approve investments up to
S$500,000 for listed vendors. Meridian was not a listed vendor.

MR. NAIR SC: But you said Mr. Lee's authority applies to "listed vendors."
Was that an oral policy or in writing?
PW1: It was in the internal policies document dated 2020. Exhibit P12.

MR. NAIR SC: Looking at Exhibit P12, the relevant section reads: "The
Managing Director may approve payments to existing counterparties up
to S$500,000 without board approval." It says "payments" and "existing
counterparties" — not "investments" and "listed vendors" as you stated.
PW1: The spirit of the provision —
MR. NAIR SC: The document says what it says. Thank you. No further questions.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF DW1 (The Accused — Mr. Winston Lee Beng Huat)

MR. NAIR SC: Mr. Winston, can you explain the transfers to Meridian
Venture Capital?
DW1: Meridian was a business development partner. We were exploring
a joint venture in offshore marine services in Indonesia. The transfers
were advance payments and capital contributions for this potential
venture. I discussed this with our board chairman, Mr. David Tan Ah Kow,
on several occasions.

MR. NAIR SC: Was there documentation of this understanding?
DW1: Yes. Email exchanges between myself and David Tan. I have provided
these as Exhibit D9.

MR. NAIR SC: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF DW1

SDPP HENG: Mr. Winston, the director of Meridian Venture Capital is
Philip Tan Ah Kow. You refer to your chairman as David Tan Ah Kow.
Are Philip and David the same person?
DW1: No. Philip is David's son. Philip was running the venture on
his father's behalf.

SDPP HENG: This is the first time in these proceedings that you have
mentioned Philip Tan being David Tan's son. This relationship does
not appear in your police statements, your Case for the Defence, or
your witness list. Can you explain this omission?
DW1: I assumed it was common knowledge among those involved.

SDPP HENG: Your email exchanges, Exhibit D9 — I note these emails
are between you and an address "david.tan@pinnaclemarine.com.sg." That
is an internal Pinnacle email address. If David Tan was representing
Meridian — an external company — why were these discussions on internal
email?
DW1: We were exploring whether Meridian would become an internal
investment vehicle. It was informal at that stage.

SDPP HENG: The metadata of Exhibit D9, as analysed by the prosecution's
forensics expert in Exhibit P18, shows all emails in the chain were
created on the same computer on 3 October 2024 — after these proceedings
were initiated. Can you explain why emails you claim were sent between
2022 and 2024 were all created on one day in October 2024?
DW1: I do not understand metadata. I printed them from my email.

SDPP HENG: The metadata shows they were created on your personal
MacBook Air, not a mail server. They appear to have been composed,
not received.
DW1: That is not correct.

SDPP HENG: No further questions.

COURT: The court notes the following significant contradictions:
(1) The accused described the chairman's name as "David Tan Ah Kow"
but the Meridian director is "Philip Tan Ah Kow" — a distinction
the accused disclosed only during cross-examination, not in prior
statements.
(2) The forensic metadata evidence in Exhibit P18 indicates the
email chain in Exhibit D9 may have been fabricated on a single day
after these proceedings began. This is a serious issue going to
the heart of the defence.
(3) The prosecution's policy interpretation ("existing counterparties")
and the defence's interpretation ("listed vendors") differ — the
court will construe the document.
