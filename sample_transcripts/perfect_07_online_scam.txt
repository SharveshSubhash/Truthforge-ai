DISTRICT COURT OF SINGAPORE
CRIMINAL CASE NO. DC/CC 191/2025

PUBLIC PROSECUTOR v HAN WEI QIANG

Date of Hearing: 21 April 2025
Coram: District Judge Chong Soo Lim
Counsel for Prosecution: Deputy Public Prosecutor Mr. Lim Boon Teck
Counsel for Defence: Mr. Steven Lee Han Jun

CASE SUMMARY:
The accused is charged under s.420 read with s.34 of the Penal Code for
cheating as part of a syndicate. It is alleged that between October and
December 2024, the accused participated in a fake online shopping scam,
operating as a money mule receiving and transferring fraudulently obtained
funds. The accused received a total of S$47,200 across 14 transactions.
The accused claims he thought he was doing legitimate remittance work.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF PW1 (First Prosecution Witness — Ms. Patricia Tan
Siew Choo, victim, 52 years old, accounts clerk)

DPP LIM: Ms. Patricia, what happened to you?
PW1: In November 2024, I saw an Instagram advertisement for branded bags
at 80% discount. I placed orders for three bags totalling $2,460. I
transferred money to a PayNow number as instructed. No bags arrived.
When I tried to contact the seller, the Instagram account was gone.

DPP LIM: Did you receive any of your money back?
PW1: The police later told me the PayNow number received my payment and
transferred it out the same day. Nothing was recovered for me.

DPP LIM: No further questions.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF PW2 (Second Prosecution Witness — Inspector Lim
Jin Long, Commercial Affairs Department, Singapore Police Force)

DPP LIM: Inspector Lim, can you describe the accused's role?
PW2: The accused received 14 separate transfers from 11 different victims
across October to December 2024, totalling S$47,200. On each occasion
the money was received into his OCBC account, Exhibit P4, and then
immediately transferred — within minutes — to two overseas accounts in
Thailand and Vietnam.

DPP LIM: What was the accused told about the work?
PW2: The accused's own WhatsApp messages, Exhibit P5, show conversations
with a contact named "Boss Tony" in which Boss Tony explained: "Your
job — receive money, send it. Take 3% each time." The accused replied:
"Okay but if police ask?" Boss Tony said: "Just say you don't know."
The accused replied: "Okay."

DPP LIM: Were warnings given to the accused?
PW2: Yes. The OCBC bank sent the accused an SMS warning on 3 November 2024:
"Unusual activity detected. Do not transfer funds if requested by strangers.
This may be a scam." The accused continued to receive and transfer funds
for another 6 weeks after receiving this warning.

DPP LIM: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF PW2

MR. LEE: Inspector Lim, "Boss Tony" — has he been identified and charged?
PW2: Investigations are ongoing. We believe Boss Tony is overseas.
The accused is one member of a larger syndicate.

MR. LEE: The accused's 3% commission — how much did he actually keep?
PW2: Approximately $1,416 across 14 transactions.

MR. LEE: No further questions.

---

EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF OF DW1 (The Accused — Mr. Han Wei Qiang)

MR. LEE: Mr. Han, did you know you were participating in a scam?
DW1: I did not know. Boss Tony told me it was a legal remittance service
for workers who cannot use banks easily. I thought it was like Western
Union, just informal.

MR. LEE: What about the bank SMS warning you received?
DW1: I saw it but I thought it was a generic warning that banks send to
everyone. I did not think it applied to my situation.

MR. LEE: No further questions.

---

CROSS-EXAMINATION OF DW1

DPP LIM: Mr. Han, Boss Tony told you "if police ask, just say you don't
know." You agreed. If you genuinely believed this was legitimate
remittance work, why would you need a cover story for the police?
DW1: I — I was just following instructions. I didn't think about it.

DPP LIM: Is it standard in legitimate remittance work to be told what
to say to the police?
DW1: No.

DPP LIM: The 14 victims who transferred money to your account all lost
money purchasing goods that never existed. You transferred their money
overseas within minutes. You received a clear bank warning. You had
a rehearsed police denial ready. Given all of this, is it your evidence
that you genuinely did not suspect anything was wrong?
DW1: I trusted Boss Tony. I was naive.

DPP LIM: No further questions.

COURT: The prosecution evidence is consistent and compelling:
- PW1 (victim): Clear account of being defrauded; PayNow linked to
  accused's account.
- PW2 (CAD officer): 14 traced transfers totalling $47,200; WhatsApp
  evidence showing accused was explicitly briefed to lie to police.
- Bank warning SMS: Accused continued operations 6 weeks after explicit
  warning.
- No material inconsistencies in the prosecution case.
The accused's claimed naivety is difficult to reconcile with the
WhatsApp instruction to deny knowledge to police, which the accused
accepted without objection.
